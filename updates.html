March 2026 Research

KLF5 results are in — and they're pointing somewhere real.

After months of monitoring our 50-mouse cohort through IVIS imaging, we're starting to see a signal. Preliminary results suggest that KLF5 overexpression accelerates primary tumor progression and increases metastatic burden — while knockouts show the opposite. This positions KLF5 as both a potential prognostic biomarker and a therapeutic target.

There's still a lot of analysis ahead, but seeing functional in vivo data line up with the molecular predictions from our CUT&RUN work is genuinely exciting.

When I started this project, one of the harder decisions was how to prioritize what to measure. In a discussion with my mentor, I proposed capturing both terminal metastatic burden (a cleaner endpoint) and longitudinal tumor kinetics via repeated IVIS imaging. We went with both — and that decision is now paying off because we can see when the divergence begins, not just where it ends up.

Next steps involve more detailed histopathological analysis of the tumor samples, and we're planning to validate the KLF5 expression changes with additional molecular work. I'm also starting to write up the methods section for what will hopefully be a manuscript later this year.

February 2026 Personal

Why I started this fundraiser — and what it actually pays for.

A few people have asked me why I'm doing this publicly instead of just applying for more grants. The honest answer: research grants fund the science — reagents, animal care, sequencing runs. They don't fund the scientist doing it.

Graduate researchers at funded programs typically receive a stipend of $25,000–35,000 a year. I do equivalent work without that support. Every day I drive to UCSF's Mission Bay campus costs me $150/month in parking alone — on top of student loans, a failing laptop, and application fees that topped $2,000 this cycle. None of that is a personal expense. It's the hidden cost of doing science without institutional backing.

MD/PhD applications cost money — application fees across programs can easily exceed $2,000. Conference travel isn't always covered. Thesis-year expenses pile up. And unlike some of my peers, I don't have a financial safety net.

I'm not asking for charity. I'm asking for a bridge — one that lets me focus on the work instead of calculating whether I can afford to apply to one more program or attend one more conference. Every bit helps, and I'm genuinely grateful for anyone who reads this far.

January 2026 Community

I started a club, and it already has 30 members.

I founded SOMA — the Systems-Oriented Medical Anatomy Club — at SFSU this semester, and the response exceeded what I imagined. Thirty students showed up to the first meeting, including pre-med, pre-PA, nursing, and biology students who don't usually share a learning space.

The goal is simple: connect anatomy to its clinical meaning. We run workshops on things like what actually happens during a thoracotomy, or how anatomical variation affects surgical planning. It's the kind of learning I wish I'd had access to earlier — the kind that makes the body feel like a living system, not a list of structures to memorize.

What I didn't anticipate was how much students were craving this kind of cross-disciplinary community. We've had students bring clinical cases from their shadowing hours, students who want to present research, and students who just want a room where it's okay to ask "but why does that actually matter?"

Running SOMA alongside my research and teaching load is a lot — but it's also the most energizing thing I do each week. The club is a reminder of why mentorship matters: because someone has to create the spaces that didn't exist when you needed them.

February 2026 Personal

My first publication is out in Cancer Discovery.

The paper is officially published. I'm listed as a co-author on a study mapping epigenetic and transcriptional programs that define osteosarcoma subtypes — work I contributed to through immunofluorescence experiments and IHC methods sections.

Seeing my name on a Cancer Discovery paper is something I genuinely didn't know was possible for someone like me two years ago. It matters — not just on a CV, but as proof that the path I'm on is real.
